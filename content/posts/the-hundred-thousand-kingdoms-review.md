**Rating: 9/10**

If you've read enough fantasy, you know the feeling. Another medieval kingdom, another chosen one, another magic system that feels like a reskin of the last three books you read. The Hundred Thousand Kingdoms isn't that.

N.K. Jemisin built something genuinely original here. The setting alone sets it apart: gods (actual gods) enslaved and living among mortals, wielded as weapons by the ruling family. It's a premise that sounds wild on paper and somehow works even better on the page.

What really got me, though, were the grief themes. Itempas, one of the enslaved gods, is processing grief in the most destructive way imaginable. It's messy and raw and very human for a being that's supposed to be above all that.

There's also something to be said about the slavery parallels. The book doesn't hit you over the head with it, but the echoes of real-world history are there if you're looking. Gods stripped of agency, forced to serve, their power turned against their will. It's uncomfortable in the way good fiction should be.

If you're tired of fantasy that plays it safe, this one's for you.
